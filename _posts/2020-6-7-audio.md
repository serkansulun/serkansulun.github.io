Here is a toy example that demonstrates the power of generative adversarial networks (GANs) for audio generation, similar to the examples of [video frame prediction]({{ site.baseurl }}{% link _posts/2020-5-27-lfp.md %}). Generating long samples of audio is much more difficult than predicting a video frame given its past frames, hence the samples provided below have very low perceptive quality. State-of-the-art audio generation models such as [Wavenet](https://arxiv.org/abs/1609.03499) creates audio signals with a short length, heavy quantization, and at a very slow rate, generating one audio sample at a time. 

This toy experiment is branched off from my existing work on deep learned audio enhancement with no change in network architecture. Admittedly, it is a very bad way to perform audio generation, as the provided samples also demonstrate. The experiment is solely performed to show the effect of adversarial learning on audio generation. It aims at predicting an audio chunk with a length of 8192, given the previous chunk with the same length. The longer audio clips below are not created recursively, meaning that while predicting each chunk, the provided previous chunk is taken from ground-truth, similar to a video codec. 

The main comparison is between the neural networks that are trained with a mean-squared loss only (baseline) versus adversarial and mean-squared losses combined (GAN). Even though GAN yields a higher quantitative error, the outputs are somehow sharper and more realistic. To equalize the sound levels of ground-truth and outputs, the network outputs are amplified by 20 decibels.
